The Internet: a thing so commonly used nowadays

The Internet is, in the present day, a very commonly present library of links available at your fingertips. Some might even say it's a necessity. But how did we get here?

The Past

It all started in the 1950's, where computers with barely any memory were taking up whole rooms. Soon enough, progress took over and we were already having proper compilers, programs and the likes in the 60's. ARPANET was the first successful networking project and in 1969 the first message was typed and sent.

But this was all experimental and for private (like ARPANET) or military use mostly. The first commercial hyperlinked applications (so the definition of a "web") started appearing in the 1980's, and as the chains of accessibility were to be broken, the first web browser -the "World Wide Web"- was created by 1989(Tim Berners-Lee, 1989).

Following the next couple of years, he worked on to making different protocols and languages, namely HTTP and HTML, to lay the base for the first ever proper web system, and founded the W3C. Following his creation, Mosaic was released in 1993, and in 1994 Netscape released their own browser, widely used then. Microsoft proceeded on to releasing their own browser, taking upwards to 75% of the market due to their bundling of the browser with Windows, marking the start of the Browser Wars between them and Netscape. In that meantime, HTML was progressing through different iterations and changes, such as HTML 4.0 or nearly being replaced by XHTML in the 90's.

The Present

Following the war, many competitors emerged against Windows' Internet Explorer, such as Opera, Safari and Chrome through the 90's and 2000's. Netscape renamed their browser to Mozilla within that period, and Internet Explorer became Microsoft Edge in 2015. Many browsers with different benefits are nowadays available to all users with different tastes and needs. Meanwhile, the W3C adapted in 2014 HTML5 as the universally admitted markup language for websites, and has been used ever since.

The Future

Progress is inevitable, but it seems that HTML5 at it's core is here to stay. Updates are being applied to browsers to improve overall user experience, which seems to be the main focus for now. Who knows what the future holds?
